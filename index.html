Selfie: The Wrong Way vs. The Right Way

Have you ever taken a selfie up close and noticed your nose looks way too big or your face just looks kinda weird? That’s because when you hold the camera really close, the lens makes whatever is closest to it look bigger. So your nose, which is sticking out, gets exaggerated while your ears might look smaller. It’s like a funhouse mirror effect! But here’s a cool trick: step back a few feet from the person, zoom in with your camera, and then take the picture. Try to keep the face the same size in the photo. This way, everything is more evenly away from the lens. There’s no weird stretching or shrinking of your features. That’s why the first picture looks so much more natural—it than the last one shows your face the way it really is, not all distorted. So next time, take a step back and zoom in for a better photo!

Architectural Perspective Compression

When you take a picture of a long street from far away with your camera zoomed in, the buildings and objects in the photo look like they are very close together. The street seems shorter and everything looks flat. This is because the zoom lens makes faraway things look bigger, but it also makes the space between them look smaller. Now, if you walk closer to the scene and take another picture without zooming, the view looks very different. The street appears longer, and you can see more space between the buildings. Things in the front look bigger, and things in the back look smaller. This is how our eyes normally see the world. The reason for this difference is how the camera lens works. Zooming in from far away compresses the view. It makes objects at different distances look like they are all at the same distance. When you take a picture up close without zoom, the perspective looks more normal and deep. That’s why the two photos of the same street can look so different!

part3

The Dolly Zoom

There is a special camera effect called the dolly zoom. Sometimes it's called the "Vertigo shot" because it was used in an old movie named Vertigo. Here's how it works: you move the camera backward while zooming in at the same time. The background seems to change while the main subject stays the same size. This happens because moving and zooming at the same time changes how we see the space around the subject.
